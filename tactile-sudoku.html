Tactile Sudoku

03

Exploring how touch, contrast, and clear structure can make puzzles easier to navigate — especially when vision isn’t the primary sense.

6 weeks (Mar – Apr 2026) · In progress

Tools: Claude, Arduino, Autodesk Fusion

Team project

CONTEXT

Sudoku is a popular puzzle game, but traditionally relies on your vision to solve it. Tactile adaptations exist, but are still thin on flexibility and feedback; screen readers or a helper change the quiet, solitary rhythm that makes the game what it is.

GOAL

Our goal was to rebuild the puzzle for touch, so the game can be played without vision.

Tactile intent

A physical 6×6 Sudoku board designed so players with low vision can enjoy the game independently.

The board is designed around touch. Instead of numbers, it uses 6 distinct shapes, each with its own texture, making them easy to recognize by touch. Textured navigation buttons make it easy to explore the grid and track where you are on the board.

Unlike paper adaptations of tactile Sudoku, this board provides feedback through vibration and optional sound. Vibration confirms placements and flags issues; sound gives an additional clue for solving the puzzle.

The result is a tactile, replayable interactive Sudoku experience that allows for independent play.

KEY IDEA

Accessible Sudoku rebuilt for touch, where tactile play deepens its calm, grounding feel.

More about the process

PROTOTYPING

Clay on a gridded board — fast iteration before PCB and sensing.

Air-dry clay and a simple grid tested whether six distinguishable pieces and clear cell edges were enough to play before locking in fabrication. Side-button layouts went through several texture and size passes — shape alone isn’t always enough when hands are already mapping space.

ONLINE SIMULATION

A lightweight online simulation helped check rules and feedback logic away from the hardware while the physical prototype evolved.

Open the simulator →

FABRICATION

Copper-clad fibreglass field — traces, contacts, and magnets in mind.

The playing surface is a soldered copper board: conductive paths and contacts where pawns meet the grid. Magnets in the pieces pair with embedded hardware so pieces seat firmly and stay easy to explore by hand.

Bench work — prep, soldering, sanding — closed the gap between layout drawings and what the sensing layer actually needed.

ELECTRONICS

Resistor in each pawn → firmware maps reads to six “digits.”

Each pawn carries a resistor with a unique value; contact with the copper field lets the firmware tell which shape sits in a cell — no camera, no RFID — so the board can track the puzzle state for checks and feedback.

  • Calibrate ranges so adjacent values don’t bleed together at the edge of tolerance.
  • Tame electrical bleed between rows and columns — signal isolation is the main hardware fight on a dense grid.
  • Stress-test placement, lift, and re-seat so reads stay trustworthy in real play.
